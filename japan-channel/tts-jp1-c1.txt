Deep in the forests of southern Japan, there's an island where trees have been alive for over 7,000 years. Some are older than the Pyramids of Giza. Older than Stonehenge. Older than written human history itself.
Welcome to Yakushima — one of Japan's most mysterious and sacred places. In this video, we'll explore the hidden wonders of Japan that most tourists never see.
Yakushima is a small island off the southern coast of Kyushu. It's a UNESCO World Heritage site, home to the Yakusugi — ancient cedar trees that are thousands of years old.
The most famous is Jomon Sugi, estimated to be between 2,000 and 7,000 years old. Its exact age is unknown because the tree is so old that its core has decayed, making carbon dating impossible.
Reaching Jomon Sugi requires a 10-hour round-trip hike through some of the most beautiful forest on Earth. The trail takes you through moss-covered rocks, crystal-clear streams, and dense ancient woodland.
The forest of Yakushima inspired Hayao Miyazaki's masterpiece "Princess Mononoke." If the forest in that film looks familiar, that's because it was drawn from real life.
Yakushima is also one of the rainiest places on Earth. It's said to rain here "35 days a month" — a Japanese joke meaning it rains almost constantly. This rainfall creates an ecosystem unlike anywhere else.
From ancient forests to hot springs. About 300 kilometers northeast of Yakushima, there's a place where monkeys do something you wouldn't expect.
At Jigokudani Monkey Park in Nagano, Japanese macaques — known as snow monkeys — bathe in natural hot springs during winter. They're the only non-human primates in the world that do this.
The behavior started around 1960 when a young female monkey began sitting in an outdoor hot spring. Others copied her, and now it's a cultural tradition passed down through generations of monkeys.
The park is only accessible by a 30-minute walk through snowy forest. In deep winter, temperatures drop to minus 10 degrees Celsius. But the monkeys sit comfortably in 40-degree water, completely unbothered.
It's a uniquely Japanese experience — watching wild animals practice the same onsen culture that humans have enjoyed for centuries.
If you visit Japan in summer, there's one sound you'll never forget.
The Japanese cicada, or semi, produces one of the loudest insect sounds on Earth. Some species reach 120 decibels — as loud as a rock concert.
There are over 30 species of cicada in Japan. Each has a different song. The tsukutsukuboshi sounds like its name. The minminzemi has a melodic whistle. And the kuma-zemi — the bear cicada — sounds like a screaming chain saw.
In Japanese culture, the cicada's song is deeply nostalgic. It represents summer, childhood, and the fleeting beauty of life — a concept called mono no aware.
About 20 kilometers off the coast of Nagasaki, there's an island that looks like a battleship. It's called Hashima — or Gunkanjima, "Battleship Island."
In the 1950s, this tiny island was the most densely populated place on Earth. 5,000 people lived in a concrete fortress just 480 meters long and 150 meters wide.
They worked in an undersea coal mine that extended over 1,000 meters below the seabed. When petroleum replaced coal in 1974, the mine closed. Within months, the island was completely abandoned.
For decades, Hashima was a ghost island — forbidden to visitors. Today, it's a UNESCO site and open for tours. But the decaying buildings and empty hallways still feel like a scene from a post-apocalyptic film.
In fact, it appeared in the James Bond film Skyfall as the villain's hideout. And if you visit, you'll understand why — the island has an eerie, otherworldly atmosphere.
It's a reminder of Japan's rapid industrialization and the impermanence of human achievement.
From ancient forests and hot-spring-loving monkeys to singing insects and abandoned cities — Japan is full of hidden wonders.
