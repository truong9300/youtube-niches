What if I told you that you could get free money from the Australian government just by saving for retirement?
Not a tax deduction. Not a rebate. Free cash deposited into your super account. No strings attached.
And yet, fewer than 25% of eligible Australians actually claim it. That means millions of dollars in free money is left on the table every single year.
In this video, I'll explain exactly how the government's Super Co-Contribution works and how you can claim yours.
The Super Co-Contribution is a government program where the ATO deposits money directly into your super account if you make after-tax contributions.
For every dollar you contribute from your after-tax income, the government will add up to 50 cents. The maximum co-contribution is $500 per year.
So if you put in $1,000 of your own money after tax, the government adds $500. Total: $1,500 going into your super. A free 50% return instantly.
This is not a tax deduction. You don't need to owe tax. You just need to have earned income and put after-tax money into super.
So who qualifies? Let's break it down.
Criterion one: Your total income must be under $58,445 per year. The full $500 is available if you earn under $45,000. Between $45,000 and $58,445, it phases out gradually.
For every dollar you earn over $45,000, the co-contribution reduces by 3.33 cents. At $58,445, it reaches zero.
Criterion two: You must make after-tax (non-concessional) contributions to your super. This is money you've already paid tax on. You can't claim the co-contribution on salary sacrifice or employer contributions.
Criterion three: You must be under 71 years old and meet the work test, which means you're employed and earning at least 10% of your income from employment or business.
And one more: your total super balance must be under $1.9 million. But let's be honest — if your super is over $1.9 million, you probably don't need the $500.
Let me show you how this works with a real example. Meet Emma. She's 28, works as a casual in retail, and earns $38,000 a year.
Emma decides to put $1,000 of after-tax savings into her super. Because she earns under $45,000, she gets the full $500 co-contribution.
That $1,500 invested at 7% for 30 years before retirement becomes $11,400. From an out-of-pocket cost of just $1,000.
If Emma does this every year from age 28 to 60, that's 32 years × $500 = $16,000 in free government money. Invested, it grows to over $70,000.
$70,000. For doing something that takes 10 minutes on your banking app.
How do you actually do it? Two ways.
Option one: Log into your super fund's online portal. Most have a "Make a contribution" button. Enter your bank details, choose after-tax contribution, and send the money. It takes 2 minutes.
Option two: Use BPAY. Your super fund has a BPAY biller code and reference number on their website or app. Send money directly from your bank.
The key thing: make sure you select "non-concessional" or "after-tax" contribution. If your super fund gets it wrong and codes it as before-tax, the ATO won't know you made an eligible contribution.
Here's the best part: you don't need to apply for the co-contribution. The ATO automatically calculates it when you lodge your tax return. If eligible, the money appears in your super account within 60 days of you filing.
And there's a related benefit if you're married or in a de facto relationship.
If you contribute after-tax money to your spouse's super and they earn under $40,000, you can claim a tax offset of up to $540.
Your spouse's super contribution gets the co-contribution too. So a low-income couple could receive up to $1,000 in co-contributions plus $540 in tax offsets. That's $1,540 in government benefits from $4,000 in contributions.
Mistake one: accidentally making before-tax contributions. Check your fund's payment instructions carefully. Use "non-concessional" or "after-tax" wording.
Mistake two: missing the deadline. Contributions must be received by your super fund before June 30 to count for that financial year.
