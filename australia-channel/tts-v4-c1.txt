Two Australians. Both 65. Both worked their whole lives. One retired with $800,000 in super. The other retired with $80,000.
The first one travels, dines out, helps their kids with a house deposit. The second relies entirely on the Age Pension. What made the difference?
In this video, we'll answer the single most important question: how much super do you ACTUALLY need to retire in Australia in 2026?
For years, the Association of Super Funds of Australia — ASFA — has published benchmarks.
For a single person, ASFA says you need $595,000 for a modest retirement and $690,000 for a comfortable one. Couples need $690K and $790K respectively.
But here's the problem: those numbers were calculated before the cost-of-living crisis. Before rent skyrocketed. Before energy bills doubled. In 2026, you need more.
After adjusting for inflation and the cost-of-living crisis, here are the real numbers for 2026:
For a single person you need roughly $750,000 to $850,000 for a comfortable retirement. For a couple: $900,000 to $1,000,000.
A comfortable retirement means: you own your home outright, you can afford private health insurance, a reasonable car, a domestic holiday once a year, and eating out occasionally. Not luxury — comfortable.
Modest means: you own your home, you have basic health cover, limited travel, and you watch your spending.
Financial planners use the 4% rule. The idea is simple: in retirement, you can safely withdraw 4% of your super balance each year without running out of money for 30 years.
So if you have $800,000, you can withdraw $32,000 per year. Combined with a part Age Pension, that's roughly $900 per week for a single person.
If you want $50,000 a year in retirement income, you need $50,000 ÷ 4% = $1,250,000. That's your target number.
Here's a simple calculator. Take the annual income you want in retirement — say $45,000. Divide by 0.04. That gives you $1,125,000 — your super target.
But here's what most people don't realise — the Age Pension exists. And for most Australians, it will be part of your retirement income.
In 2026, the full Age Pension is about $28,600 a year for a single person and $43,000 for a couple.
But the pension is means-tested. The more super and assets you have, the less pension you get.
For a single homeowner: you start losing pension when your assets exceed $301,750. You lose it entirely at $674,000. For couples: $451,500 threshold, $1,012,500 cut-off.
The sweet spot for many Australians is having $400,000 to $600,000 in super plus a part Age Pension. This provides around $45,000-$55,000 a year in total income — very comfortable.

Let's bring this home. Here's how to figure out your personal situation.
Step one: log into your super account and check your current balance.
Step two: pick your target from the table we showed earlier. $750K for modest, $850K for comfortable.
Step three: use a compound interest calculator. The ATO has one, or just Google "super calculator." Enter your current balance, your age, your salary, and your target. It will tell you if you're on track.
