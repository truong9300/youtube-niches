Imagine you're working your 9-to-5 in Sydney, Melbourne, or Brisbane. Every time you get paid, a chunk of your salary — 11.5% — disappears into a account you can't touch until you're 60.
It's called Superannuation. Australians have over $4 trillion dollars sitting in these accounts. But here's the crazy part — most people have no idea how it works. And that ignorance is costing them hundreds of thousands of dollars.
In this video, I'll explain exactly how Australian Super works in 2026, how you can maximise it, and the common mistakes that could cost you your retirement.
Let's start at the beginning. Superannuation isn't a choice. It's mandatory.
Back in 1992, Prime Minister Paul Keating introduced the Superannuation Guarantee. The idea was simple — force employers to put money aside for workers' retirement, so people wouldn't rely solely on the Age Pension.
In 1992, it started at 3% of your salary. By 2002, it was 9%. Today, in 2026, it's 11.5% — and it's scheduled to rise to 12% by 2028.
Here's what most people get wrong: this 11.5% is paid by YOUR EMPLOYER on top of your salary. It's not taken out of your take-home pay. It's extra money going into your super fund, invested in shares, property, and bonds, growing over decades.
Let's put some numbers on this. Say you're 25 years old, earning the Australian average salary of $98,000 a year.
Your employer puts in 11.5% — that's $11,270 a year. Over 40 years, assuming 7% average investment return, that single year's contribution grows to over $240,000 by the time you retire.
Now multiply that by 40 years of work. We're talking over $2.4 million in today's dollars. That's the power of compound interest over a working lifetime.
But here's the kicker — if you don't understand how super works, you could be leaving hundreds of thousands on the table.
In Australia, there are three main types of super funds.
First, Industry Super Funds. These are run by unions and industry bodies — not-for-profit. Names like AustralianSuper, Hostplus, and HESTA. They tend to have lower fees and historically better returns. Around 40% of Australians choose these.
Second, Retail Super Funds. These are run by banks and financial institutions — for-profit. AMP, MLC, Colonial First State. They charge higher fees, but offer more products and advice. About 30% of Australians use these.
Third, Self-Managed Super Funds — SMSFs. You run your own fund, choose your own investments. Only 5% of Australians do this — it's for people with at least $200,000 in super who want direct control.
The difference in fees between an industry fund and a retail fund might not sound like much — 0.9% versus 1.3%. But on a $500,000 balance over 30 years, that 0.4% difference costs you over $150,000.
Now let's talk about the most powerful tool in the Australian super system — salary sacrifice.
Here's how it works. When you ask your employer to put extra pre-tax money into super, it's taxed at only 15% instead of your marginal tax rate — which could be 32.5%, 37%, or even 45%.
The cap for these concessional contributions in 2026 is $30,000 per year, including your employer's 11.5%. So you can add roughly $18,500 extra yourself.
And if you didn't use your full cap in previous years, you can use unused caps going back 5 years. This is called the carry-forward rule, and it's massively underutilised.
Here's a free money hack that most low-income Australians don't know about.
If you earn under $58,445 a year and make after-tax contributions to your super, the government will match 50 cents for every dollar you put in — up to $500 free.
